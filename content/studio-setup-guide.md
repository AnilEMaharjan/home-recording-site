# The $300 Home Recording Studio — Complete Beginner's Guide

**Everything you need to record quality audio at home, without wasting money on gear you don't need.**

---

## Before You Buy Anything

Most beginners make the same mistake: they buy gear before they understand what each piece does. This guide fixes that. Read it once before you spend a dollar.

**What this guide covers:**
- The exact gear to buy at three budget levels ($100 / $200 / $300)
- What order to buy things in
- The one mistake that ruins 80% of beginner recordings (it's not the gear)
- A room treatment checklist you can do for under $30
- Settings to dial in on your first session

---

## What You Actually Need (And What You Don't)

### You need:
- A microphone
- An audio interface (if using an XLR mic)
- Headphones
- A DAW (recording software)
- A quiet-enough room

### You don't need:
- A mixer (this is for live sound, not bedroom recording)
- Expensive cables (a $10 XLR cable sounds identical to a $60 one)
- Acoustic foam on every wall
- A pop filter right away (a curved hand in front of the mic works fine to start)
- Monitors (headphones are better in an untreated room)

## The $100 Setup — Absolute Beginner, USB Only

**Best for:** Podcasters, streamers, anyone who wants plug-and-play with no extra gear.

| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| Samson Q2U (USB + XLR mic) | ~$60 |
| Free DAW: GarageBand (Mac) or Cakewalk (Windows) | $0 |
| **Total** | **~$60** |

The Samson Q2U connects directly to your computer via USB — no interface needed. It also has an XLR output, so if you decide to upgrade later, you keep the mic and just add an interface. Smart first buy.

**What you're missing at this level:**
- Headphone monitoring (use your existing headphones via the mic's headphone jack)
- Sound isolation (find the quietest room in your place, record with windows closed)

## The $200 Setup — First Real Studio

**Best for:** Musicians recording guitar/vocals, serious podcasters, anyone who wants XLR quality.

| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| Audio-Technica AT2020 (XLR condenser mic) | ~$99 |
| Focusrite Scarlett Solo (audio interface) | ~$120 |
| Free DAW: GarageBand or Cakewalk | $0 |

This is the setup that will hold up for years. The AT2020 is a genuinely good condenser mic — not "good for the price," just good. The Scarlett Solo is the most recommended beginner interface for a reason: it works, the preamps are clean, and it comes with software worth more than the interface (Ableton Live Lite + plugins).

**What you're missing at this level:**
- Dedicated headphones (borrow or use earbuds for now)
- Room treatment

## The Room: The Thing Nobody Talks About

Your room matters more than your gear. A $99 mic in a good room sounds better than a $500 mic in a bad room.

**What makes a bad recording room:**
- Hard, parallel walls (creates flutter echo and comb filtering)
- Big empty spaces (long reverb tails)
- Street noise, HVAC, refrigerator hum

**What you can do for free:**
- Record in a closet full of clothes (best free acoustic treatment there is)
- Hang heavy blankets on walls behind and beside you
- Put a pillow or folded blanket behind the mic to absorb reflections
- Turn off HVAC during takes, record with windows closed

**What you can buy for under $30:**
- A pack of 12 acoustic foam panels (~$20–25 on Amazon) placed on the wall behind the mic and the wall behind you
- Note: foam panels reduce high-frequency reflections, not low-frequency rumble. They help with echo, not with road noise.

## Gain Staging: The Mistake That Ruins Most Beginner Recordings

This is the most common issue and almost nobody teaches it clearly.

**The problem:** Your recordings sound quiet, or you crank the gain and get distortion and hiss.

**The fix:**
1. Set the gain knob on your interface so that your loudest moments peak around **-12dB to -6dB** in your DAW. Not 0dB, not -30dB.
2. If you're peaking below -20dB, turn the interface gain up.
3. If you're hitting 0dB or clipping (turning red), turn it down.
4. Never touch your computer's input volume — use only the interface gain knob.

**How to check in GarageBand:** Watch the level meter on the track. Aim for the meter to hit yellow briefly on your loudest moments. If it's barely moving, you need more gain. If it's hitting red, back off.

## First Session Checklist

Before you press record, run through this:

**Room:**
- [ ] Windows closed
- [ ] HVAC/fans off
- [ ] Phone on silent
- [ ] Computer notifications muted

**Hardware:**
- [ ] Interface plugged in via USB, lights on
- [ ] Mic plugged into XLR input (input 1)
- [ ] Headphones plugged into interface headphone jack (not computer)
- [ ] 48V phantom power ON if using a condenser mic (AT2020 needs this)
- [ ] Monitor mix knob set to center (blend of input and playback)

**Software (GarageBand / Cakewalk):**
- [ ] New project created
- [ ] Input source set to your interface (not "Built-in Microphone")
- [ ] Sample rate: 44.1 kHz or 48 kHz
- [ ] Bit depth: 24-bit
- [ ] Recording armed on the track

**Gain:**
- [ ] Record a test line at your loudest expected volume
- [ ] Peak level hitting -12dB to -6dB on the meter
- [ ] No red clipping indicators

**Then:** Record a 30-second test, play it back through headphones. Listen for:
- Hiss (reduce gain, move mic closer)
- Room echo (find a deader space, add absorption behind you)
- Hum/buzz (check cable connections, move away from computer power supply)

## Upgrade Path: What to Buy Next

Once you've recorded for 3–6 months and want to improve:

1. **Better headphones or monitors** — if you're mixing music, open-back headphones (Sennheiser HD 560S ~$150) improve stereo imaging significantly
2. **Acoustic treatment** — bass traps in corners ($50–100) before more foam panels
3. **Better mic** — the AT2020 will hold you back less than you think; upgrade your room first
4. **Paid DAW** — Logic Pro ($199, Mac) is the natural GarageBand upgrade; Ableton Live Intro ($99) for beat-based production
